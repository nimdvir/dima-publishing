# Purpose of the Lab

This lab transfers the Chapter 3 Let's Build logic from the Grading Database to PetVax. Students use a spreadsheet because Chapter 3 focuses on data fundamentals, not Access or SQL yet.

The lab is designed to show that spreadsheets are useful but fragile when data is repeated, connected through formulas, edited inconsistently, and asked to represent multiple business entities in one flat file.

The key bridge to Chapter 4 is vocabulary:

- redundancy;
- update anomaly;
- insertion anomaly;
- deletion anomaly;
- query fragility.

## Question 13

**Brightspace type:** Multiple Choice  
**Correct answer:** B — A primary key, such as a `PetID` assigned to each pet.

## Question 14

**Brightspace type:** Multi-Select  
**Correct answers:** A, B, C, and E

Co-ownership in a flat file forces the entire pet record (name, breed, weight) to be repeated for each owner, which produces redundancy (A), update drift (C), and identity ambiguity (E). The flat file also cannot record the second owner without duplicating the pet record (B). Option D is wrong — the workbook does not automatically link the two owners to one pet.

> **Brightspace setup:** mark A, B, C, and E as the correct selections and D as incorrect. Use the all-or-nothing (exact match) grading option so the full set must be selected for credit.

# Instructor Notes

## Why this lab works

This lab mirrors the Chapter 3 Let's Build structure but transfers it to PetVax. The Let's Build has students use spreadsheets to experience fields, records, data types, metadata, filters, lookups, pivot tables, and spreadsheet fragility. This lab does the same with PetVax appointments.

## Why the two-part grading model

The Brightspace quiz carries the concept scoring with exact, auto-graded answers, so grading is fast and consistent. The file submission is a completion-and-authenticity check: it confirms the student actually performed the spreadsheet steps behind their answers, without forcing the instructor to re-score every cell by hand.

## Why this lab prepares Chapter 4

Students experience the exact problems that motivate databases:

- repeated owner and pet facts;
- inconsistent updates;
- inability to store pets or owners independently from appointments;
- accidental loss of entity information when appointment rows are deleted;
- fragile formulas and fixed ranges;
- weak lookup relationships.

That gives Chapter 4 a clear purpose: databases are not introduced because spreadsheets are bad. Databases are introduced because related, reusable, shared business data needs stronger structure.
