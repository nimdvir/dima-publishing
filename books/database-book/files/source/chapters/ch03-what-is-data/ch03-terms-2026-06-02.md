| Term / Concept | Definition | Business Significance | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | A data quality dimension that measures whether a stored value correctly reflects the real-world fact it represents. | Inaccurate data leads to wrong reports, bad forecasts, and poor decisions — no matter how polished the dashboard looks. | A student earned 92 but 29 was entered; a product price listed as $9.99 instead of $99.90. |
| **Big Data** | Data characterized by extreme volume, velocity, and variety that exceeds the capacity of traditional tools to store, process, and analyze efficiently. | Enables large-scale pattern discovery, personalization, and real-time decision-making, but magnifies the cost of poor data fundamentals. | A retailer analyzing billions of clickstream records; a hospital combining sensor data, lab results, and visit notes. |
| **Bit** | The smallest unit of digital data, represented as a 0 or a 1. | Bits are the atomic building blocks of all stored data; understanding them grounds the data hierarchy. | Eight bits form one byte, enough to represent the letter `A` in ASCII. |
| **Byte** | A group of 8 bits, commonly used to represent one character or small unit of digital information. | Bytes are the first level at which data becomes human-meaningful — a single typed character. | The word `Score` uses 5 bytes; `StudentID` uses 9 bytes. |
| **Categorical Data** | Data that places observations into labels or groups rather than measuring them as quantities. | Determines which analyses are valid: categorical fields support grouping and counting, not averaging. | `DeliverableType` (Quiz, Homework, Exam); `Region` (Northeast, Southeast); `PaymentMethod` (Card, Cash). |
| **Completeness** | A data quality dimension that measures whether all required data is present and nothing critical is missing. | Incomplete data creates blind spots: missing attendance records or scores make performance summaries unreliable. | A gradebook with 5 blank `Score` cells out of 80; a customer list missing phone numbers for 20% of contacts. |
| **Consistency** | A data quality dimension that measures whether the same fact is represented the same way across records, systems, or time. | Inconsistent data makes aggregation and comparison impossible — two departments report different revenue for the same quarter. | One record says `Quiz`, another says `quiz`; a customer listed as `Jane Smith` and `J. Smith` in the same system. |
| **Data Anomalies** | Structural problems in flat files caused by mixing multiple themes in one table: redundancy (same fact stored many times), update anomalies (changing one copy misses others), insertion anomalies (no place to add a new fact without unrelated data), and deletion anomalies (removing one fact accidentally removes another). | Anomalies make data fragile, error-prone, and hard to maintain — they are the primary reason organizations move from spreadsheets to databases. | In a flat gradebook, Alice`s email appears on every grade row; changing it on one row leaves stale copies elsewhere. Adding a new student with no grades yet has no clean place to go. Deleting the only grade for Daniel also deletes Daniel. |
| **Data Dictionary** | A formal record of shared field definitions, meanings, formats, allowed values, and usage rules. | A data dictionary turns field names into shared understanding; without it, every analyst and system interprets fields differently. | A dictionary entry for `StudentID`: "Unique identifier, format S####, never averaged." The LB`s `DATA_DICTIONARY` tab. |
| **Data Governance** | The system of roles, standards, policies, and processes used to manage data consistently and accountably over time. | Governance answers who owns the data, who can change it, and how changes are approved — without it, data quality always slips back. | A university policy that only the Registrar`s office can update student enrollment status; a retail chain`s data stewardship committee. |
| **Data Hierarchy** | The progression from bits to bytes to fields to records to tables to databases, where each level adds more structure and meaning. | The hierarchy explains how raw binary storage becomes organized business information — it is the conceptual backbone of database design. | A `Score` of 92 starts as bits, becomes a byte, lives in a field, sits in a record with other fields, belongs to a table of grades, and is managed inside a database. |
| **Data Lifecycle** | The sequence of stages through which data passes: collection, storage, cleaning, integration, use, retention, archiving, anonymization, and deletion. | Lifecycle thinking prevents data from becoming a liability — old, unowned datasets create cost, risk, and clutter. | A grading dataset is collected each semester, used for advising, retained per university policy, and eventually anonymized or deleted. |
| **Data Type** | The classification of a stored value that determines its valid operations, acceptable formats, and storage behavior. | Choosing the wrong type breaks calculations, sorting, filtering, and joins — a `Score` stored as text cannot be averaged. | Integer for counts, Date/Time for deadlines, Short Text for names, Boolean for attendance (Present/Absent). |
| **Field** | A single stored attribute within a table column — one piece of data about one thing. | Fields are the atomic unit of structured data; every query, filter, and report operates on fields. | `StudentID`, `Score`, `DueDate`, and `FirstName` are each one field in the Grading Database. |
| **Flat File** | A single, non-relational data structure that stores rows of values without built-in support for relationships across multiple datasets. | Flat files are easy to create but become fragile as data grows — they are the starting point most organizations outgrow. | A CSV file containing student names, grades, and attendance all in one sheet; a single exported Excel worksheet. |
| **GIGO** | Garbage In, Garbage Out — the principle that flawed, inconsistent, or poorly defined input data produces flawed results no matter how sophisticated the analysis. | GIGO is a manager`s warning: investing in dashboards and AI without investing in data fundamentals wastes both money and trust. | A beautifully formatted KPI dashboard built on data where `Quiz` and `quiz` are treated as different categories. |
| **Interval Data** | Data with equal, meaningful intervals between values but no true zero point; calendar dates and temperature in Celsius are common examples. | Interval data supports addition, subtraction, and averaging, but ratios are meaningless — you cannot say one date is "twice" another. | `DueDate` values: the gap between March 1 and March 8 is the same as between March 8 and March 15, but March 1 is not "half" of March 2. |
| **Metadata** | Data about data — information that explains what stored values mean, what format they follow, what rules apply, and how they connect to other data. | Metadata makes data interpretable by people other than its original creator and by systems that need to validate and transform it. | A dropdown list on `DeliverableType` that limits entries to Quiz/Homework/Exam/Project; a comment on `DueDate` clarifying it means "original deadline, not submission date." |
| **Nominal Data** | Data that labels categories without implying any order, rank, or amount. | Nominal fields support counting and grouping but not ranking — confusing them with ordinal or numerical fields produces meaningless results. | `DeliverableType` (Quiz, Homework, Exam, Project); `PaymentMethod` (Card, Cash, Transfer); `Region`. |
| **NULL** | A marker that means "no value has been stored" — distinct from zero, an empty string, or a blank space. | NULL is the most misunderstood value in databases; filtering, joining, and aggregating all behave differently when NULL is present. | A `Score` cell left empty because the grade has not been entered yet (NULL) vs. a score of 0 (the student earned zero points). SQL uses `IS NULL` to find these rows. |
| **Numerical Data** | Data that represents quantities and can support arithmetic operations when the measurement level allows it. | Numerical data powers totals, averages, trends, and forecasts — but only when the field truly measures an amount, not just an identifier. | `Score` (0–100), `Revenue`, `UnitsSold` are numerical; `StudentID` looks numerical but is categorical. |
| **Ordinal Data** | Data that places categories in a meaningful rank order but without equal or known distances between ranks. | Ordinal data supports ranking and median comparisons, but averaging is misleading because the gaps between levels are not equal. | A satisfaction survey scale (1 = Very Dissatisfied to 5 = Very Satisfied); letter grades (A, B, C, D, F). |
| **Primary Key** | A field (or combination of fields) that uniquely identifies each record in a table. | Primary keys are the foundation of reliable data — they ensure every record can be found, referenced, and connected without ambiguity. | `StudentID` in the Students table; `DeliverableID` in the Deliverables table. |
| **Qualitative Data** | Data that describes qualities, labels, or characteristics rather than measurable amounts. | Qualitative data explains why something happened; it supports root-cause analysis, customer understanding, and contextual decision-making. | An instructor comment: "Great improvement on the second attempt"; a customer review: "The checkout process was confusing." |
| **Quantitative Data** | Data that represents measurable quantities — counts, amounts, durations, or scores. | Quantitative data supports numerical analysis, KPIs, and performance measurement across every business function. | A quiz score of 88; 142 units sold; 3.5 hours of screen time; $4,250 in monthly revenue. |
| **Ratio Data** | Data with equal intervals and a meaningful true zero point, making all arithmetic operations — including ratios — valid. | Ratio data is the most analytically powerful measurement level; it supports full arithmetic, percentages, and multiplicative comparisons. | `Score` (0 means no points earned; 80 is twice 40); `Revenue` ($0 means no revenue); `UnitsSold`. |
| **Record** | A complete row of related field values describing one instance of something — one student, one transaction, one grade. | Records are the basic unit of business data; every report, query, and analysis starts from individual records. | One row in `GRADEBOOK`: Alice Johnson, Quiz 1, Database Basics, 92 — that is one record. |
| **Semi-Structured Data** | Data that has some organizational markers such as tags, key-value pairs, or hierarchies but does not follow a rigid table schema. | Semi-structured data is common in web services, APIs, and configuration files; organizations often extract structured tables from it for analysis. | JSON from a web API: `{"StudentID": "S1048", "Score": 92}`; XML configuration files. |
| **Structured Data** | Data that fits a predefined format with clearly named fields, consistent types, and a known arrangement of rows and columns. | Structured data is the foundation of relational databases, SQL, and most business reporting — it is the main focus of this course. | A `GRADEBOOK` table with fixed columns (RecordID, StudentID, Score, etc.) where every row follows the same schema. |
| **Table** | A structured collection of records in which each row represents one instance and each column represents one clearly defined attribute. | Tables are the organizing principle of databases — separating data into well-designed tables is what prevents anomalies and enables reliable queries. | The `STUDENT` table (one row per student), the `GRADES` table (one row per grade), the `DELIVERABLES` table (one row per assignment type). |
| **Timeliness** | A data quality dimension that measures whether data is current and available when needed for decision-making. | Late data is nearly as useless as wrong data — a dashboard built on week-old numbers drives week-old decisions. | Attendance recorded the same day vs. scores entered two weeks after the deadline; a sales report refreshed hourly vs. monthly. |
| **Uniqueness** | A data quality dimension that measures whether each real-world entity appears only once in a dataset — no unintended duplicates. | Duplicate records inflate counts, distort averages, and waste resources — one customer looks like three in a marketing campaign. | A student listed as both `Jane Smith` and `Jane M. Smith`; a product entered twice under slightly different SKUs. |
| **Unstructured Data** | Data with no predefined schema or organized structure — emails, images, audio, video, PDFs, and free-text notes. | Unstructured data holds valuable insight (customer sentiment, medical images, contract text) but requires extra processing before it can be analyzed in tables. | A professor`s written feedback on an essay; a scanned invoice; a security camera recording; a customer service call transcript. |
| **Validity** | A data quality dimension that measures whether stored values follow defined rules, formats, ranges, and expectations. | Invalid data breaks calculations, blocks automation, and requires manual cleanup before any analysis can be trusted. | A `Score` of 105 when the valid range is 0–100; a `DueDate` of `13/25/2026`; a `StudentID` that does not match the `S####` format. |

## Acronyms and Abbreviations

| Abbreviation | Full Form | Brief Meaning | Where It Appears |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CSV** | Comma-Separated Values | A plain-text flat-file format that uses commas to separate fields and line breaks to separate records. | Flat file and data exchange discussion; imported/exported in Google Sheets and database tools. |
| **GIGO** | Garbage In, Garbage Out | The principle that flawed input produces flawed output regardless of tool quality. | Data quality discussion; reinforced in the LB when case drift breaks pivots and VLOOKUPs. |
| **NOIR** | Nominal, Ordinal, Interval, Ratio | A mnemonic for the four measurement levels, from least to most analytically powerful. | Measurement levels section; the LB quick-classify mini-check. |
