## Chapter Overview

Every business runs on questions. How many orders shipped late last month? Which
products sell best on weekends? Which customers are about to leave? The answers
live in data, and the systems that organize that data are databases.

This chapter sets up the rest of the book. We start with the difference between
data and information, then look at why structured storage matters once a business
grows past a single spreadsheet.

## Why Databases Matter

A spreadsheet works fine for a short list. It breaks down when many people need
the same data at once, when the data must stay consistent, and when one mistake
can ripple across thousands of rows.

A database solves three problems a spreadsheet cannot:

- **Shared access** so many users work from the same source.
- **Consistency** so the same fact is not stored five different ways.
- **Scale** so performance holds as records grow into the millions.

## Key Concepts

We will return to these ideas in every chapter:

- **Data** are raw facts. **Information** is data placed in context.
- **Tables** store data in rows and columns.
- **Relationships** connect tables so data is not repeated.
- **Queries** ask questions of the data.

In the next chapter we connect these ideas to business performance and the role
of information systems.
